Place Information

Hampi, a UNESCO World Heritage Site located in the Indian state of Karnataka, is one of the most extraordinary historical and archaeological sites in India, once the prosperous capital of the Vijayanagara Empire, which was among the richest and most powerful empires in the world during the 14th to 16th centuries. Nestled along the banks of the Tungabhadra River, Hampi sprawls across a surreal boulder-strewn landscape, interspersed with the magnificent ruins of temples, palaces, markets, fortifications, aqueducts, and ancient monuments that offer a glimpse into the grandeur of a bygone era. Founded in 1336 by Harihara I and Bukka Raya I, the Vijayanagara Empire rose to become a cultural and economic powerhouse in South India, and Hampi served as its flourishing capital for over two centuries. During its peak under the reign of Emperor Krishnadevaraya in the 16th century, Hampi was a thriving cosmopolitan city, teeming with wealth, art, literature, and spiritual activity, attracting traders, travelers, and scholars from across India, Persia, Arabia, and Europe. The architectural brilliance of Hampi is reflected in its numerous monuments, the most famous being the Virupaksha Temple, which continues to function as a living temple and is dedicated to Lord Shiva. Its towering gopuram, pillared halls, intricate carvings, and grand courtyards exemplify the Dravidian architectural style.
